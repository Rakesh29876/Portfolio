MTA Ridership Dashboard

Data analytics excel Project

  1. Home
  2. Mta Ridership Dashboard

Introduction:

This project presents a comprehensive analysis of MTA ridership data using Microsoft Excel, utilizing Pivot Tables, slicers, and charts to derive meaningful insights into commuter patterns and transit usage. The dataset includes ridership data segmented by stations, subway lines, hours, weekdays, months, and passenger counts. The primary goal is to identify trends in passenger flow, peak travel times, and station popularity to support data-driven decisions for optimizing transit operations and improving service efficiency.

Data Preparation:

  1. Data Loading:

    The dataset is loaded into Power Query for further analysis.

  2. Data Type Adjustment:

    Data types are modified to ensure consistency and accuracy in subsequent analyses.

  3. Column Removal:

    The "neighbourhood" column, containing only null values, is removed to streamline the dataset.

  4. Price Formatting:

    The price column is formatted as currency to enhance readability.

  5. Date Extraction:

    Year and month are extracted from the "last review date" column to facilitate temporal analysis.

Pivot Tables:

Pivot tables are created to summarize and visualize key insights:

  1. Total Ridership and Trip KPIs:

    Measures total passenger entries (footfall), total number of trips made, average passengers per trip, and average transfers per passenger.

  2. Hourly Ridership:

    Visualizes passenger counts by hour, highlighting peak commute times around 8 AM and 5 PM, when ridership surges due to work travel.

  3. Ridership by Weekday:

    Shows fairly consistent daily ridership during weekdays, with a noticeable increase on Saturdays and Sundays, reflecting higher leisure and tourism travel on weekends.

  4. Ridership by Line or Route:

    Breaks down total ridership by subway lines or bus routes, showing which transit corridors carry the most passengers, such as the 1, 2, 3 subway lines or major bus lines.

  5. Top 5 Stations by Ridership:

    Highlights the busiest stations including Times Square–42nd Street, Grand Central, and 34th Street–Penn Station, pinpointing key hubs that experience the highest passenger volume.

Pivot Charts:

Charts were created to visually represent insights from pivot tables:

  1. Bar Chart: Ridership by Weekday.
  2. Line Chart: Ridership Trend by Hour/Month.
  3. 3D Column Chart: Yearly Subway Comparison.
  4. Column Chart: Ridership by Region/Service.

Results:

  1. Peak Ridership Hour:

    Most subway entries occur around 8 AM, aligning with typical morning commuter rush hours. This highlights the critical need for optimal train frequency and staffing during early mornings to manage passenger flow efficiently.

  2. Top Station:

    Times Square–42nd Street station sees the highest ridership, indicating its importance as a major transit hub and transfer point. This station requires prioritized maintenance and crowd control measures.

  3. Line Usage Leaders:

    The 1, 2, 3 lines dominate ridership figures, reflecting their extensive coverage of key residential and commercial areas. Attention to these lines for capacity upgrades can improve overall system performance.

  4. Ridership by Day:

    Weekday ridership remains consistently high, peaking during Monday to Friday commutes. There is a slight but steady increase on weekends, likely driven by leisure travelers and tourists, suggesting the need for balanced service levels throughout the week.

  5. Passenger Preferences:

    Data shows higher ridership for express trains during peak hours, signaling passenger preference for faster travel options. This can inform scheduling to maximize express train availability when demand is greatest.
